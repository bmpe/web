Development of expansion wave generator
for shock wave therapy

Introduction

    In addition to conventional application, ESWL, the effects of shock waves on biological soft tissues have attracted attention recently. The increase of capillaries and the blood flow was observed after shock wave radiation. Also, the cell growth can be controlled by shock waves. However, the mechanisms of those new effects remain unknown. 
    "Expansion waves" are considered to be a significant factor for cellular effects above. Expansion waves, which decrease the pressure at the points they pass, affect cells differently from compression waves, because they exert an extension force on cells. However, the effects of an expansion force with a short duration (as shock waves) on the cells are unknown. Therefore, fundamental studies are needed to understand these effects, especially on cells.
    With this background, we developed an expansion wave generator, which was capable of an application to experiments with cells.

Generation of expansion wave

where the pressure of the incident wave is pi, pressure of the reflected wave is pr, acoustic impedance of the medium on the incidence side is z1, and acoustic impedance of the medium on the transmission side is z2. This equation shows that the reflectance from a medium with higher acoustic impedance to one with lower impedance is negative. When a shock wave is reflected at such a surface, the reflected wave is believed to become an expansion wave.
    Figure 1 shows the configuration of our system. The system consisted of an ellipsoidal reflector, a PDMS (SYLGARD®184, Dow Corning Toray Co., Ltd.) unit, a pair of electrodes and a high-voltage power supply (HVPS) to generate spherical shock waves. The PDMS unit was used to reflect shock waves generated in the reflector. The acoustic impedance of PDMS is much larger than that of air. Therefore, the shock wave was reflected at the PMDS-air surface and became an expansion wave.

Visualization of expansion wave

    We used a schlieren imaging technique to visualize the flow in order to understand the wave in perspective. We observed waves with a high-speed camera (HPV-2, Shimadzu Co., Ltd) using a shooting speed of 1 Mfps (1 µs/frame) and an exposure time of 0.25 µs.
    The schlieren photographs near the second focal point are shown in Figure 2. First, direct wave that was not reflected at the reflector was observed 24 μs after the discharge. Second, the focused wave that passed the second focal point was observed approximately 25 µs after the discharge (white arrow in Figure 2, 25 μs).

Figure 2: Visualized expansion wave
Upside of the surface center of the picture is air, and downside is water.
The number lower right is time after discharge [μs].

Pressure measurement

　To measure the waveform, a hydrophone (Müller-Platte Needle Probe, Dr. Müller Instruments) was used. The peak pressure was −5.52 ± 1.23 MPa (n=10) with discharged voltage 4kV. The typical waveform is shown in Figure 3.

Figure 3: Typical waveform of expansion wave

Conclusion

    We proposed and developed an expansion wave generator with shock wave reflection. Thorough visualization and pressure measurement, the generation and focusing of expansion wave were observed.
