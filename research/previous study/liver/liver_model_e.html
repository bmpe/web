Image-Based Biomechanical Simulation for
Computer Aided Liver Therapies

Purpose

　An objective of this research is to develop a multilevel model of liver - virtual model that can establishes connection between the mechanical properties of the whole liver (macromechanics) and that of the liver lobules and cells (micromechanics). The development of this model is important to simulation of advanced therapies such as molecular medicine that requires accurate approximation of chemical and electrochemical phenomenal as well as the mechanics. A clinically viable simulation will enable us to better understand and evaluate the procedure, and hence, develop new device, plan localisation and customise procedure for individual patient.

Methods

　As the first step to construct the simulation system, we conducted experimental study on liver biomechanical properties and investigate constitutive relations for liver tissues from macroscopic point of view. In our experiments, we used a test instrument to measure the force-displacement of liver specimens during compression and then elongation (Figure 1). Specimens were extracted from fresh livers collected at local slaughterhouse. Experimental results were analysed to obtain constitutive equations for Finite Element (FE) analysis of liver deformation.

　We are developing a microscopic measurement system to measure liver properties at cellular levels. Meshfree method is used to model the dynamics-like behaviours at this level, and then blended with a mainly continuum description of the surrounding material in the liver organ level. Figure 2(a) shows the finite element model of liver organ completed with hepatic arteries and veins and Figure 2(b) is our simplified model of liver lobule and cells. The liver organ model is developed in collaboration with the Biomechanical Labs, University of Tokyo.

　Our PC-based simulation system comprises an augmented reality workbench overlaying patient images with dynamic models. The workbench has conventional stereoscopic views and 3D interaction tools. A library of generic models is parameterised to fit specific patient liver from his/her diagnostic MR/CT images. Simulation of mechanical deformation of liver from electrodes introduction can be demonstrated using the simulation system in its current form.

Conclusion

　We believe that key to a clinical viable simulation of emerging therapies is the availability of a unified multilevel liver model that represents both cellular and organ levels in the biological organisation. Different in dominating phenomenal at these levels needs a constitutive equation that integrates electrical, biochemical and mechanical phenomenal. To integrate biomechanics and molecular biology is very challenging but necessary to address questions neither field alone can do.
