Intraoperative precise diagnosis and ablation of brain tumor using fluorescence spectra

Brain tumor operation

　Glioma is a kind of brain tumor arising from glial cells in a brain. Though higher resection rate of tumor brings good patient prognosis, tumor operation is difficult because of properties of infiltration of malignant tumor. In other words, as there is mixed region of tumor cells and normal brain cells, complete resection by conventional surgery is very difficult. Recent developments in computerized imaging and navigation systems provide great benefit for surgery. Especial-ly, Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) guided navigation has high advantage of tumor detection and resection. However, as a brain moves at craniotomy (called brain shift), there are some registration errors between MRI image and the actual brain. Although some hospitals equip intraoperative MRI, which can take MRI images during surgery, clinical utilization is limited because of the large system.

Tumor identificaition by fluorescence

　As an alternative tumor detection method, there is fluorescence guided surgery which uses autofluorescences or endogenous fluorescence materials. In clinical study, 5-Aminolaevulinic acid (5-ALA) induced Protoporphyrin IX (PpIX) fluorescence has begun to be used in the past decade. 5-ALA specifically accumulates at malignant glioma tissues and PpIX that is a metabolite of 5-ALA emits red fluorescence under violet excitation light.

Precise identification using fluorescence spectra

A recent study showed that PpIX spectrum shape is important for precise tumor detection and malignancy diagno-sis. Also, some group revealed that there are differences of autofluorescence spectrums between tumor and normal brain. Although these studies found characteristic spectrums of tumor comparing spectrums with pathological results, the comparison method in these studies have the following limitations: There is a possibility that a spectrum measurement point is different from a pathology assessment point. In order to solve above problem, we aimed to develop a local spectrum measurement system and create a method to precisely compare spectrums with pathological results using developed system.

Fig.2 Spectrum scaninng system

Comparison of spectra with pathological results

　We measured brain tumor tissues using the system that we developed.

　Spectrums of 754 points from 13 samples were acquired (two glioblastoma multiform, one anaplastic oligodendroglioma, one anaplastic oligodendro-astrocytoma and one oligodendroglioma). Fig.3 showes one of results acquired from a patient of glioblastoma multiform.

Fig.3 Comparison of spectra with pathological results
left: histological preparation　　center: Intensity distribution of PpIX　　right: Intensity distribution of autofluorescence

Blue shading means regions which include tumor cells and yellow shading means a region which includes reactive cells. PpIX intensity is well corresponding to tumous region. Especially, the local region on the right side also emits strong PpIX fluorescence. This result suggests that the local accumulation of tumor can be identified by PpIX fluorescence. Furthermore, distribution of autofluorescence (the intensity at 585 nm) showed opposite distribution to the one of PpIX. This light may be derived from autofluorescent substances such as NADH and flavins.

In this way, we precisely compared spectrum with pathological results with a novel system. Our results lead to not only for the precise identification of brain tumor but also further development of PDD (Photodynamic Diagnosis).

Precise ablation by a laser system

　We also developed a laser ablation system for precise resection of brain tumor. An Er-YAG laser at the wavelength around 3μm was used for this system in order to ablate only the surface layer of tissues because absorption of water was very high at the wavelength.

Integration of spectrum measurement and laser ablation

　Fig.4 shows an autofocus surgical microscope equipped with a spectrum measurement system and an ablating laser. This system can automatically focus to the point indicated by a red guide laser and ablate broader area moving the microscope by XY stages after scanning of fluorescence spectra. However, because the system is huge, it is difficult to use at the clinical use.

Fig.4 An autofocus surgical microscope equipped with a spectrum measurement system and an ablating laser

　To solve above problem, we also developed a scanning system using galvano mirrors. This system consist of a fluorescent measuring part, a scanning part using galvano mirrors and a computer for controlling. Measurement and ablation can be performed coaxically by changing golden mirror fixed inside the system. Fig.6 shows a ablated pocine brain by our system.
