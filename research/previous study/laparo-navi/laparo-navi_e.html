Development of Laparo-Navigator

Introduction

　Laparoscopic surgery rapidly evolves as minimally invasive surgery. The internal anatomy is observed through the laparoscope and the procedures are performed using long instruments that are inserted through a trocar. Compared with open surgery, this surgery is more difficult and the manipulation of the laparoscope is important for smooth work. Since the surgeon usually uses both hands to manipulate long instruments, the assistant (camera- man) must hold the laparoscope. In many cases, it is difficult for the assistant to steadily hold the laparoscope and keep the view upright. Especially for beginners, it is extremely difficult to aim the scope at the spot where the surgeon needs it.

　In the past, several engineers have proposed laparoscope manipulators to solve these problems. But these systems don't have sufficient safety and cannot be sterilized. These points are necessary for the clinical use of such an electrically controlled manipulator. To realize sufficient safety and possibility of sterilize, we developed a new type of robotic laparoscope manipulator system (Laparo-Navigator) that could follow surgeon's wishes without getting in the way of the surgeon's manipulation of the instruments.

System Construction

　Fig.1 shows the system construction of the Laparo-Navigator system.　It consists of a Laparoscope Manipulator and a man-machine interface. The surgeon watches the TV monitor which show the laparoscopic view through a CCD camera and controls the manipulator.

Fig.1 The system configuration of manipulator for laparoscopic surgery

Mechanism of the Manipulator

　We used a five-bar linkage mechanism for the Laparoscope manipulator(Fig.2). In this setup, the laparoscope is attached to a holder with two degrees of angular freedom near the insertion site of the abdomen and the camera end is held by the manipulator with five-bar linkage mechanism. The manipulator movement in the X-Y plane changes the angle of the laparoscope. For back and forth movements, we used an optical zooming.

Fig.2 Mechanism of laparoscope manipulator using a five-bar linkage mechanism

　The advantages of this mechanism are as follows,

  • Since the manipulator can be placed far away from the patient's abdomen, the manipulator imposes minimum obstruction to the work of the surgeon.
  • Moving range is mechanically limited and the mechanism does not have redundant degrees of freedom. Although this may be a disadvantage for general industrial robots, it become merits for manipulators for medical use. Because it avoids unexpected movements.
  • This mechanism has enough stability to give the laparoscope steady movements.
  • Electrical devices such as motors are set bellow, so that upper links can be removed easily from the controller. Consequently, the controller device can be covered with drape and the linkage part is sterilized.

Man-machine interface

　We have developed several man-machine interface for the Laparo-Nvigator system.

  • Voice Recognition System
  • Head Mouse using a head mounted gyro sensor
  • Hand switch attached on forceps

　Fig.4 shows the system configuration of the Head Mouse. It consists of a gyro sensor, a knee switch and an indicator. We used a gyro sensor (Virtual Reality Sensor GU-3011, Datatec) mounted on the surgeon's head to measure the surgeon's head angle. Three-dimensional angular and angular velocity data are sent to a PC from the gyro sensor via RS232C.

　To move the manipulator, the surgeon starts by switching on the knee switch. At that moment, the gyro sensor data is calibrated. The surgeon then moves his/her head in the direction in which he/she wants to move the laparoscopic image. For example, if the surgeon wants to move the laparoscope image to the right, he/she turns his/her head to the right. The indicator indicates the direction in which the manipulator is going to move. After checking the indication lamp, the surgeon turns the knee switch to execute the move.

　Because the manipulator is using the surgeon's head angle, it is easy for the surgeon to control the manipulator, and his/her work is not obstructed by the system. The most important feature of this system is its safety. This is achieved by the use of a knee switch and an indicator. The use of a knee switch avoids confusion with other switches. The surgeon must confirm the input displayed by the indicator before any motion is executed.

Fig.4 System construction of the "Head-mouse" system using a gyro sensor and a knee switch

Clinical application

　We have started the clinical application on the manipulator(Fig.5). The operation was completed safely. The manipulator did not obstruct the surgeon's operation. As a man-machine interface, we used the hands witch.
