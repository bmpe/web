Determining Depth of Fluorescent Inclusion in Tissue

Based on Fluorescence Spectra Change

Introduction

　 Fluorescence imaging have received particular attention in surgery due to increasing availability of fluorophore loaded reporters for noninvasively pinpointing small disease foci and for monitoring cellular biochemical process in vivo [1]. However, in most clinical applications, fluorescent imaging is limited to epi-illumination method [2] which is difficult to resolve depth. Therefore, various fluorescence molecular tomography (FMT) have been developed to extract depth information from the arrival time of photons [3] or the spatial distribution of fluorescence intensity [4]. However, to obtain temporally resolved or spatially resolved information, a streak camera or multiple source-detector pairs is always required respectively, leading tomography of these kinds to high-cost and complicated system. As an alternate, spectrally resolved FMT [5] was proposed based on the hypothesis that fluorescence spectra provide depth information of fluorescent inclusion in tissue [6]. In this research, we evaluated the potential of spectra info in determining the location of fluorophore in tissue.

Method

    A phantom with an embedded fluorescent dye was made to mimic the fluorescence spectrum from a fluorophore loaded biomarker, i.e. a lesion seated in tissue. The phantom (Fig. 1) consisted of two parts: intralipid aqueous suspensions and fluorescent inclusion. Intralipid is a kind of fat emulsion widely used in optical experiments to simulate the scattering properties of tissues [7, 8]. Fluorescent dye (Alexa Fluor® 790, Invitrogen, USA) was added into a glass capillary tube (inner diameter of 1mm), which is call as “fluorescent inclusion” and located at the bottom of the phantom. By altering volume of intralipid solution, the depth of fluorescent inclusion was changed. We measured fluorescence in the trans-illumination geometry to reduce the effect of excitation light reflection. Excitation light was collimated to a beam first, and then incident on the fluorescent inclusion. Over the air-intralipid surface, a 900μm core diameter step-index fiber (N.A. = 0.22) collected fluorescence and guided it to a spectrometer (BRC642E, BWTEK, USA). A filter was used to remove transmitted excitation light from fluorescence. For excitation, we used a 790nm laser diode (CW, output: 100mW) and an 800nm long pass filter.

Fig 1. Experiment setting.

Result

    The spectra change in the NIR range was studied with an AF790 inclusion located in Intralipid 3% aqueous suspension. Different from experiments with AF647, the initial inclusion depth was set as 1mm because at 0mm transmitted excitation light was so strong that the spectrometer was statured. As shown in the Fig. 2, the spectra clearly blue shift (to the short side), as well as the intensity ratio over two bands (840-950nm to 806-840nm) decreases with the increasing depth of the AF790 inclusion. Furthermore, a linear relationship (as shown in the green line in Fig. 2(b)) exists between the ratio and the fluorophore depth up to 11mm, showing that ratio has the potential as an excellent estimator of the inclusion depth in the NIR range.

Figure 2. Spectra change in NIR. (a) Smoothed fluorescence spectra of an Alexa Fluor 790 incluison seated in intralipid 3% solution at depths from 1 to 11mm;
(b) Ratio of the integrated intensity over the longer band (Abbreviation: L, 806~840nm) to the shorter one (Abbreviation: S, 840~950nm).

Discussion

    Accurate determination of fluorophore depth requires the spectra changing significantly and sensitively. In the NIR range, the intensity ratio, as an indicator of the spectra change, linearly varies nearly 40% when the depth grows from 1mm to 11mm (Fig. 2). Therefore, it can be concluded that the spectra change can be an effective method to determine the depth of NIR fluorophore-loaded biomarker in tissue. Actually, owing to low optical attenuation in this spectral window, NIR light is capable to penetrate into tissue several centimeters [1]. Moreover, a large number of NIR targeted probes have been developed for diagnostic or therapeutic applications [2]. This spectra change method thus paves a road toward the localization of lesion located deeply in tissue with NIR fluorescent reporter.
